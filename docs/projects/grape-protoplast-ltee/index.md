# Grape Feeder Cell Mutation Accumulation

Time-series mutation accumulation experiment in grape embryogenic feeder cell suspension cultures. Sample at multiple time points → deep Illumina WGS (~100× per haplotype) → variant calling against the *Vitis vinifera* reference. Goal: characterize spontaneous mutation rate and spectrum in long-term grape cell culture, and look at allele fractions to detect subclonal mutations.

> **Disclaimer:** This document was generated by Claude (Anthropic) based on a conversation with Grey on 2026-05-02. Verify everything independently before doing anything in the lab. This is a starting point, not a validated SOP.

## Goal

Track mutation accumulation in grape embryogenic feeder cell lines over time. Sample cells at different time points, extract DNA, deep Illumina WGS (~100× per haplotype, ~200× total) to detect variants — including allele-fraction analysis to catch subclonal mutations.

The first thing to figure out: **how to reliably extract sequencing-quality DNA from these cells.** Everything else comes after that.

## What the cells are

Embryogenic callus suspension cultures from grape rootstock genotype **1103P**. Developed at the UC Davis Plant Transformation Facility by Tricoli & Debernardi (2024, *Horticulture Research*, DOI: [10.1093/hr/uhad266](https://doi.org/10.1093/hr/uhad266)).

They exist to condition the medium and provide signals that help grape protoplasts divide during CRISPR editing workflows.

Key facts:

- **Intact callus cells with cell walls** — NOT protoplasts. The protoplasts are the target cells being edited; the feeders still have walls.
- Grow in **liquid suspension** in shake flasks (90 rpm, dark, 26°C).
- Medium contains **2 g/L activated charcoal** mixed in with the cells. The charcoal adsorbs phenolic compounds and excess auxins that would otherwise inhibit embryogenesis.

## The technical problem: activated charcoal

Activated charcoal adsorbs DNA. Well-characterized — charcoal binds nucleic acids nonspecifically through hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions. **If charcoal is present when you lyse the cells, it will soak up your genomic DNA**, killing yield and potentially biasing what's recovered.

These cells live their entire lives in a charcoal slurry. There's a high probability that charcoal particles are physically stuck to cell surfaces. You cannot just pour off the medium and expect clean cells.

The dedicated extraction protocol [[grape-feeder-cell-dna-extraction]] walks through how to handle this.

## Plan of attack

1. **Charcoal separation test.** Take a small aliquot of feeder suspension. Try differential sedimentation. Check washed pellet under inverted light microscope. Try a NaCl wash if gravity alone doesn't separate cleanly. This is the first thing to figure out.
2. **Extraction test.** Once you have charcoal-free cells, extract DNA with [[qiagen-dneasy-extraction|DNeasy Plant Mini]]. Qubit + NanoDrop. Run a gel only if those look off.
3. **Pilot sequencing.** Extract DNA from 2-3 time points, send to SeqCenter or UCD DNA Technologies Core for Illumina WGS. Decide depth + budget once extraction is reproducible.

## Sequencing math

| Item | Number |
|---|---|
| *V. vinifera* genome (haploid) | ~500 Mb |
| Diploid genome size | ~1 Gb |
| Coverage target | 100× per haplotype = **200× total** |
| Data per sample | **~200 Gb** |
| NovaSeq X 10B flow cell capacity | ~3,000 Gb (3 Tb) |
| Samples per flow cell at 200× | ~15 (shared with other labs) |
| Min DNA input for Illumina DNA Prep | 100-500 ng |
| **Target yield per sample** | **≥500 ng total** (10 ng/µL × 50 µL) |
