# Agarose

## What it is

Agarose is a linear polysaccharide extracted from red seaweed (genus *Gracilaria*), made of alternating D-galactose and 3,6-anhydro-L-galactose units. When you dissolve it in boiling buffer and let it cool, the chains form a hydrogen-bonded network of pores — essentially a solid jelly with a controllable pore size.

## Why we use it

That controllable pore size is the whole point. At **0.7–1%** the pores are big enough for genomic DNA to wander through under an electric field; at **2%** they're small enough to separate short PCR products of similar length. Because DNA is uniformly negatively charged along its phosphate backbone, every fragment migrates at roughly the same force per unit length — the only thing slowing them down is how well they thread through the gel matrix. Bigger fragments get caught on more obstacles and move slower. That's how a single tube of mixed DNA fragments fans out into discrete bands on a gel. Agarose is the physical "sieve" that makes [[gel-electrophoresis]] a separation technique instead of just an electrophoretic smear.

> 💡 **Why agarose over polyacrylamide?**
> Polyacrylamide gels have tighter, more uniform pores — better for small fragments (<1 kb) and for single-base resolution. Agarose is easier to pour, safer to handle (no unpolymerized acrylamide), and covers the useful range for most cloning and genotyping work (100 bp to 20 kb). We keep both around but reach for agarose by default.

Molecular biology grade, for gel electrophoresis.
