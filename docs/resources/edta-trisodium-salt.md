# EDTA trisodium salt

## What it is

EDTA stands for **ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid** — a small organic molecule with four carboxyl groups and two amine nitrogens that, taken together, can wrap around a metal ion and grip it from six different angles. That geometry is called a hexadentate chelator, and it's why one EDTA molecule can sequester a divalent metal cation so completely that it's effectively no longer in solution. The trisodium salt is the readily-soluble form you use to make working stocks around pH 7–8.

## Why we use it

Almost every DNase and RNase enzyme on the planet requires a divalent metal cofactor — usually Mg²⁺ or Ca²⁺ — to cut a phosphodiester bond. EDTA is how we turn them off. Add EDTA to a sample, it grabs all the free Mg²⁺ and Ca²⁺, and suddenly the nucleases in your cell lysate have nothing to chew with. That's why **TE buffer** (10 mM Tris + 1 mM EDTA) is the standard storage medium for DNA, why every miniprep elution is in EDTA-containing buffer, and why every lysis buffer for DNA extraction starts with EDTA before you even get to the detergent. It's the molecular equivalent of putting your enzymes in handcuffs.

> ℹ️ **Concentration matters downstream**
> EDTA also inhibits enzymes you *want* working (PCR polymerases, restriction enzymes) by grabbing their Mg²⁺ cofactor. For anything enzymatic, dilute your template at least 10× into the reaction so the residual EDTA stays below ~0.1 mM.
