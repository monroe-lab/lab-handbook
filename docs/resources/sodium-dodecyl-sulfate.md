# Sodium dodecyl sulfate

## What it is

Sodium dodecyl sulfate — **SDS** in every lab protocol — is an ionic detergent built from a 12-carbon hydrocarbon tail and a negatively charged sulfate head group. That amphipathic shape (one end loves oil, the other loves water) is what gives it its power: SDS molecules spontaneously coat any hydrophobic surface with their tails facing the surface and their negative heads pointing out into solution.

## Why we use it

SDS is the most aggressive protein denaturant in common use, and it's ubiquitous for three related reasons. (1) **Cell lysis**: poke SDS at a membrane and the detergent intercalates into the lipid bilayer and dissolves it, releasing everything inside — the reason it's in every DNA extraction lysis buffer and most Western blot sample buffers. (2) **Protein denaturation**: above about 0.1% SDS, proteins unfold into linear chains and get uniformly decorated with SDS molecules. Since the coating is proportional to chain length, the protein's net charge ends up proportional to its mass. This is what makes SDS-PAGE sorting by molecular weight possible — without SDS, native charge and fold would dominate migration. (3) **Nuclease inactivation**: as a bonus, the same unfolding that makes proteins migratable also inactivates DNases and RNases, protecting your nucleic acid during lysis.

> ⚠️ **Respiratory irritant — weigh with care**
> SDS fine powder is a strong respiratory irritant. Always weigh in a fume hood or a ventilated weighing station, not at the open bench. The dust cloud from pouring granules into a beaker is more hazard than the liquid buffer you'll make from it.

> 💡 **Why not a non-ionic detergent?**
> Triton X-100 and NP-40 (non-ionic) are gentler — they disrupt membranes without denaturing soluble proteins, which is what you want if you're doing a native pull-down. SDS is for when "denaturing" is the whole point, as in SDS-PAGE, Southern/Northern transfers, or completely wrecking the cell proteome to get clean DNA.
