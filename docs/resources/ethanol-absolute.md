# Ethanol Absolute

## What it is

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH), also called ethyl alcohol or grain alcohol, is a small two-carbon alcohol that's miscible with water in all proportions. "Absolute" means it's been distilled to ≥99.5% purity — the remaining fraction is trace water. The 200 proof label is the US convention: proof = 2× percent alcohol, so 200 proof ≈ 100%.

## Why we use it

Ethanol shows up in almost every molecular biology workflow for two very different reasons. (1) **DNA/RNA precipitation**: at high concentrations (70–100%) ethanol reduces the effective dielectric of the solution so sodium ions can neutralize the negative phosphate backbone and the nucleic acid drops out. That's why every miniprep, phenol-chloroform extraction, and RNA workup ends with an ethanol wash. (2) **Surface sterilization**: diluted to **70%**, ethanol is actually a *better* disinfectant than absolute — the water helps it penetrate bacterial membranes before it evaporates. 100% flash-dries too fast to kill anything. This is why we keep both [[ethanol-70]] and the undiluted bottle around.

> ⚠️ **Flammability matters**
> Ethanol is a Flammable Cabinet resident for a reason — its flash point is 13 °C, well below room temperature. Don't store or open it near open flames or hot plates. Refill small working aliquots in the cabinet, not at the bench.

1 gallon bottle, 200 proof.
