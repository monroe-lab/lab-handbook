# Tris base

## What it is

Tris is short for **tris(hydroxymethyl)aminomethane** — a small organic molecule with a primary amine and three hydroxyl groups. In aqueous solution the amine can pick up or release a proton, which makes Tris a weak base: at low pH it exists mostly as Tris-H⁺ (protonated), and at high pH as the neutral free base. The pKa at 25 °C is about **8.1**, which means Tris is most effective as a buffer between roughly pH 7.2 and 9.0 — a range that conveniently covers most enzymatic and molecular biology work.

## Why we use it

A good buffer resists pH changes when you add acid or base, and Tris is the workhorse for slightly-basic conditions. It shows up in TE buffer (DNA storage), TAE and TBE (gel electrophoresis), Laemmli buffer (SDS-PAGE), transfer buffers, and wash buffers across the protocol library. Most downstream enzymes (Taq polymerase, restriction enzymes, ligase) are happiest near pH 8, and Tris is cheap, non-toxic, and doesn't interfere with UV absorbance at 260 nm the way some other buffers do.

> ⚠️ **Tris pH is temperature-sensitive**
> The pKa of Tris drops by about 0.028 per degree Celsius. A buffer you titrated to pH 8.0 at 25 °C room-temp will read pH ≈ 8.6 when you chill it to 4 °C — and vice versa. If your experiment runs at a different temperature than where you made the buffer, titrate at the use temperature or account for the shift explicitly.

> 💡 **Free base vs hydrochloride**
> This bottle is "Tris base" — just the neutral form, pH of a saturated solution is around 10–11. To make a usable buffer you dissolve Tris base in water and titrate with HCl down to your target pH. Alternatively you can buy [[1m-tris-hcl-ph-8-8-5]] pre-titrated. Either way: always set pH at the final working temperature.
