# How to Use the Scale

## Resources

**Equipment:** [[analytical-balance]] (glass-doored enclosure), [[top-loading-balance]] (open platform)

**Consumables:** [[weigh-boats]]

**Location:** [[bench-chemistry]] in [[room-robbins-0170]], near the dry chemical storage

**Related Protocols:** [[lab-orientation]], [[ms-media-recipe]], [[make-tae-buffer]], [[make-te-buffer]]

**Purpose:** Learn which balance to use, how to tare, and how to weigh solids and liquids accurately. Every buffer and media recipe starts here.

## Time estimate

**Wall time:** ~1 hr | **Hands-on:** 1 hr

## Two Balances, Two Jobs

The lab has two balances. They are not interchangeable.

### [[analytical-balance|Analytical Balance]] (glass doors)

- Has a draft shield (glass enclosure with sliding doors) to block air currents
- Reads to 0.1 mg (four decimal places in grams, e.g., 0.1234 g)
- Use for: small quantities of reagents where precision matters (enzymes, SDS, EDTA for small-volume buffers, anything under ~5 g)
- Slow to stabilize. Wait until the display stops flickering before recording
- Close the glass doors during measurement for accurate readings

### [[top-loading-balance|Top-Loading Balance]] (open platform)

- No enclosure, open weighing platform
- Reads to 0.01 g (two decimal places, e.g., 12.34 g)
- Use for: larger quantities (salts for buffers, agarose for gels, PEG, anything over ~5 g)
- Faster to stabilize
- Fine for most routine weighing

**Rule of thumb:** If the amount you need is less than 1 g and precision matters, use the analytical balance. For everything else, the top-loader is fine.

## Procedure

### 1. Check the balance

- Is it clean? If there is powder or spills on the weighing pan, clean it first with a brush or lint-free wipe.
- Is it level? Most balances have a bubble level indicator. If the bubble is off-center, do not adjust it yourself. Ask Grey or your mentor.
- Is it turned on and reading zero? If not, let it warm up for a few minutes.

### 2. Tare

Taring sets the display to zero so you can subtract the weight of your container.

1. Place your empty container ([[weigh-boats|weigh boat]], beaker, [[falcon-tubes-50ml|Falcon tube]]) on the pan.
2. Press the **Tare** button. The display should read 0.000 g (or 0.00 g on the top-loader).
3. Now add your reagent. The display shows only the weight of the reagent, not the container.

**Always tare.** Never try to do mental subtraction. It is the most common source of weighing errors.

### 3. Weigh your reagent

- For powders: use a clean spatula. Add slowly. It is easier to add a tiny bit more than to remove excess.
- For liquids: pipette or pour into the tared container on the balance. Do not pipette directly over the balance pan.
- For volatile reagents: work quickly. Some reagents (like [[2-mercaptoethanol|beta-mercaptoethanol]]) evaporate and the reading will drift downward.

### 4. Record and clean up

- Record the actual weight you measured, not the target weight. If the recipe calls for 4.4 g and you weighed 4.38 g, write 4.38 g. For most protocols, this is close enough. For critical measurements, adjust.
- Return the reagent bottle to its correct storage location (chemical cabinet, shelf, fridge).
- Brush off any powder from the weighing pan and the area around the balance.
- If you spilled anything, clean it up completely. See [[spill-response]] if it's a hazardous chemical.

## Common Errors

| Error | What happens | How to avoid |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Forgetting to tare | You include the container weight in your measurement | Always tare. Always. |
| Weighing with the analytical balance doors open | Air currents cause the reading to fluctuate | Close the doors, wait for stability |
| Adding too fast | Overshoot the target weight | Add in small increments, especially near the target |
| Using a dirty spatula | Contaminate the reagent bottle | Use a clean spatula for each reagent. Never double-dip. |
| Not returning the reagent bottle | Next person can't find it | Put it back where it came from |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Tare and weigh a solid

1. Tare an empty weigh boat on the analytical balance.
2. Weigh exactly 0.500 g of any available non-hazardous salt ([[sodium-chloride|NaCl]] is fine).
3. Record your actual measurement. How close did you get?
4. Repeat until you can hit within 0.005 g of the target on the first try.

### Exercise 2: Top-loader for a larger amount

1. Tare a beaker on the top-loading balance.
2. Weigh 10.00 g of [[agarose]] (or NaCl, or any available dry reagent).
3. This is the amount you will weigh when making agarose gels or buffers.
