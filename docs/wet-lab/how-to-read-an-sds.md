# Chemical Lookup: How to Read an SDS

## Resources

**Related Protocols:** [[lab-orientation]], [[spill-response]], [[waste-disposal-quick-reference]]

**Purpose:** How to find a Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for any chemical in the lab, what sections matter for bench work, and when to consult one.

## Time estimate

**Reading time:** ~15 min

---

## What is an SDS

A Safety Data Sheet (formerly MSDS) is a standardized document that describes the hazards, handling, storage, and emergency procedures for a chemical. Every chemical in the lab has one. They are required by law (OSHA Hazard Communication Standard).

SDS documents follow a 16-section format mandated by the Globally Harmonized System (GHS). You do not need to read all 16 sections. For routine lab work, the sections below are what matter.

## How to Find an SDS

**Option 1: Lab inventory.** The [inventory system](../inventory-app/index.html) includes SDS search links for chemicals in the lab inventory. Click the SDS link on any chemical's inventory card.

**Option 2: Web search.** Search for `[chemical name] SDS [manufacturer]`. Example: `sodium hydroxide SDS Sigma-Aldrich`. The manufacturer's website almost always has a downloadable PDF.

**Option 3: Physical binder.** Some labs keep a printed SDS binder. Check the lab if one exists.

## Sections That Matter

### Section 2: Hazard Identification

This is the most important section. It tells you:

- **Signal word:** "Danger" (serious) or "Warning" (less serious)
- **Hazard statements (H-codes):** What the chemical can do to you (e.g., H314 = causes severe skin burns, H225 = highly flammable)
- **Pictograms:** Red-bordered diamond symbols showing the hazard class (flame, skull, exclamation mark, corrosion, etc.)

### Section 4: First Aid Measures

What to do if you or someone else is exposed:

- **Inhalation:** Move to fresh air
- **Skin contact:** Wash with water for X minutes
- **Eye contact:** Flush with water for X minutes
- **Ingestion:** Do not induce vomiting (usually), call Poison Control

### Section 5: Fire-Fighting Measures

Relevant if the chemical is flammable. Tells you which fire extinguisher type to use.

### Section 7: Handling and Storage

- How to handle safely (ventilation requirements, PPE)
- Storage conditions (temperature, incompatible chemicals to keep separate)
- This tells you which cabinet the chemical belongs in

### Section 8: Exposure Controls / PPE

What personal protective equipment you need:

- Gloves (what type: nitrile, butyl, etc.)
- Eye protection (safety glasses vs. goggles vs. face shield)
- Respiratory protection (fume hood vs. respirator)
- Lab coat

## When to Check an SDS

- **Before using a chemical for the first time.** Skim Section 2 (hazards) and Section 8 (PPE). Takes 2 minutes.
- **After a spill.** Check Section 6 (accidental release measures) for cleanup instructions.
- **If you feel sick after working with a chemical.** Check Section 4 (first aid) and Section 11 (toxicological information).
- **When storing a chemical you haven't stored before.** Check Section 7 for storage requirements.
