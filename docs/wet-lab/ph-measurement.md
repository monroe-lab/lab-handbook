# pH Measurement and Adjustment

## Background

pH measures how acidic or basic a solution is. The scale runs from 0 (extremely acidic) through 7 (neutral) to 14 (extremely basic). Most biological buffers are adjusted to a specific pH (commonly pH 5.7-5.8 for MS media, pH 7.5-8.0 for Tris-based buffers for DNA work).

**Why it matters:** Enzymes, DNA stability, and plant growth media are all pH-sensitive. MS media at the wrong pH will not solidify properly or will inhibit seedling growth. A Tris buffer at the wrong pH will give inconsistent extraction results.

## The pH Meter

The pH meter has:

- **An electrode** (a glass probe that dips into the solution). Handle it carefully. Do not touch the glass bulb at the tip. Do not let it dry out.
- **A digital display** showing the current pH reading.
- **Calibration buttons** for standardizing against known buffers.

### Electrode storage

When not in use, the electrode sits in a storage solution (usually 3 M KCl or the manufacturer's storage buffer). **Never store the electrode dry.** If the storage solution is low or dried out, refill it and tell Grey.

## Procedure

### 1. Calibrate the meter

Calibrate before each use session (or at least daily if used continuously). Calibration uses standard buffer solutions of known pH.

**Two-point calibration (standard):**

1. Rinse the electrode with distilled water. Blot gently with a lint-free wipe (do not rub).
2. Place the electrode in **pH 7.0 buffer**. Wait for the reading to stabilize.
3. Press the calibration button and confirm pH 7.0.
4. Rinse the electrode with distilled water. Blot.
5. Place the electrode in **pH 4.0 buffer** (if your target pH is acidic) or **pH 10.0 buffer** (if your target pH is basic).
6. Wait for the reading to stabilize. Press calibrate and confirm.
7. Rinse. The meter is calibrated.

**Three-point calibration** (pH 4.0, 7.0, 10.0) is more accurate but usually not necessary for routine buffer prep.

### 2. Measure pH

1. Pour your solution into a beaker (you need enough depth to submerge the electrode tip).
2. Place a stir bar in the beaker and set it on a [[stir-plate|magnetic stir plate]] at low speed. Stirring ensures a uniform reading.
3. Rinse the electrode with distilled water. Blot.
4. Submerge the electrode in the solution. The glass bulb must be fully submerged.
5. Wait for the reading to stabilize (10-30 seconds). Record the pH.

### 3. Adjust pH

Most buffers need adjustment after dissolving the components. The protocol will specify the target pH and which acid/base to use.

**Common adjustments:**

| Buffer | Target pH | Adjust with |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| MS media | 5.7-5.8 | 1 M KOH (to raise) |
| Tris-HCl (for DNA) | 8.0 | Concentrated HCl (to lower from ~10.5) |
| TE buffer | 8.0 | Usually pre-adjusted if using Tris-HCl pH 8.0 stock |
| TAE buffer | ~8.3 | Usually does not need adjustment |

**How to adjust:**

1. While stirring, add the acid or base **dropwise**. Use a pipette or a plastic transfer pipette for small additions.
2. After each addition, wait for the reading to stabilize before adding more.
3. **Go slowly near the target.** Overshooting is easy. If the target is pH 5.8 and you're at 6.2, add one drop at a time.
4. If you overshoot (went too acidic when you wanted basic, or vice versa), add the opposite reagent. But try not to overshoot; going back and forth dilutes your buffer with extra salt.

### 4. Clean up

1. Rinse the electrode with distilled water. Blot.
2. Return the electrode to its storage solution.
3. Clean the beaker, stir bar, and any pipettes used.
4. Return acid/base bottles to the [[cabinet-corrosive]].

## Common Errors

| Error | What happens | How to avoid |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Not calibrating | Readings are off, buffer is wrong pH | Calibrate every time |
| Adding acid/base too fast | Overshoot the target | Dropwise near the target |
| Not stirring | Localized pH reading, not representative | Always stir |
| Letting the electrode dry | Electrode damage, slow/inaccurate readings | Store in solution |
| Measuring in a tube instead of a beaker | Electrode can't be properly submerged | Pour into a beaker |

## Exercise

1. Calibrate the pH meter using a two-point calibration.
2. Measure the pH of distilled water. It should read close to 7.0 (often 5.5-6.5 due to dissolved CO2, which is normal).
3. Dissolve a small amount of [[tris-base]] in 50 mL of water. Measure the pH (it will be high, ~10-11). Practice adjusting it down to pH 8.0 with HCl.
