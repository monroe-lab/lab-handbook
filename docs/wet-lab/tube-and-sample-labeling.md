# Tube and Sample Labeling

## Resources

**Supplies:** Permanent markers (fine-tip Sharpie, lab markers), printed labels (if available), labeling tape, [[cryo-labels]], cryo-safe markers

**Consumables:** [[microtube]], [[pcr-strip-tubes-0-2ml]], [[falcon-tubes-15ml]], [[falcon-tubes-50ml]], [[petri-dishes-100mm]], [[autoclave-tape]]

**Related Protocols:** [[lab-orientation]], [[harvesting-leaf-tissue]]

**Purpose:** Learn the lab's labeling conventions so every tube, plate, and sample is identifiable by anyone, at any time, even after months in the freezer. Unlabeled or poorly labeled samples are useless samples.

## Time estimate

**Wall time:** ~30 min | **Hands-on:** 30 min (reading + practice)

---

## Why This Matters

An unlabeled tube in the -80C freezer is trash. You can't sequence it, you can't use it, and you can't even throw it away safely because you don't know what's in it. Every tube you create gets labeled before anything goes into it.

## What Goes on Every Tube

At minimum, every tube label must include:

1. **Date** (YYYY-MM-DD format, e.g., 2026-04-12)
2. **Your initials**
3. **Sample ID** (a unique identifier: genotype, line name, tissue type, or experiment code)
4. **Concentration** (if it's a DNA/RNA sample or a buffer stock, write the concentration)

Example for a DNA extraction:
```
2026-04-12
GM
Col-0 leaf #3
42 ng/uL
```

Example for a buffer stock:
```
2026-04-12
GM
50x TAE
```

## Labeling Rules

### Use the right marker

| Condition | Marker type | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| Room temperature | Fine-tip Sharpie | Works for most purposes |
| -20C or -80C | Cryo-safe marker or printed [[cryo-labels]] | Regular Sharpie can smear or fade at extreme cold |
| Ethanol exposure | Solvent-resistant marker | Regular Sharpie dissolves in ethanol. If your protocol involves ethanol washes, label on the cap or use printed labels |
| Autoclave | [[autoclave-tape]] + Sharpie, or printed autoclave-safe labels | Regular marker on the tube body may fade. Label the tape |

### Where to write

- **Microcentrifuge tubes (1.5 mL, 2 mL):** Write on the cap AND the side. The cap is visible when tubes are in a rack. The side is visible when tubes are in a freezer box.
- **PCR strip tubes:** Write on the caps. Use a consistent left-to-right order and record the order in your notebook.
- **Falcon tubes (15 mL, 50 mL):** Write on the frosted label area on the side.
- **Petri dishes:** Write on the bottom (agar side), not the lid. Lids can get swapped.
- **Freezer boxes:** Label the top and one side of the box with the contents, date range, and your initials.

### Freezer box grids

When storing tubes in a freezer box, maintain a grid map in the [inventory system](../inventory-app/index.html). Record which tube is in which position (e.g., A1, B3). This is critical for finding samples months later without thawing everything.

## Printed Labels

For long-term storage or large sample sets, printed labels are better than handwriting. Ask Grey about the lab's label printer and cryo-label stock. Printed labels are more legible, more durable, and include barcodes if needed.

## Naming Conventions

Use consistent, searchable names:

- **Plant lines:** Use the standard stock name (e.g., `Col-0`, `SALK_045678`, `msh2-1`)
- **Tissue type:** `leaf`, `root`, `flower`, `seed`, `whole seedling`
- **Extraction type:** `gDNA`, `HMW`, `RNA`, `nuclei`
- **Replicate number:** `#1`, `#2`, `#3` or `rep1`, `rep2`, `rep3`

**Good:** `2026-04-12 / GM / SALK_045678 leaf #2 / gDNA 38 ng/uL`
**Bad:** `sample 4` (sample 4 of what?)

## Documentation

Create a lab notebook entry. Date it. Cite this protocol. Practice labeling a few empty tubes with the conventions above. Photograph them and include the photo in your notebook entry.
