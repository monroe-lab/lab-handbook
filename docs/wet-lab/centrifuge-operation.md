## Background

A centrifuge spins samples at high speed to separate components by density. Heavier material (cell debris, precipitates) pellets at the bottom; lighter material (DNA in solution, supernatant) stays on top. Different protocols call for different speeds and tube types.

**Speed notation:** Protocols specify centrifugation as either **RPM** (revolutions per minute) or **x g** (relative centrifugal force). These are not the same. x g depends on the rotor radius. Most protocols for microcentrifuge tubes use "max speed" or "full speed" (\~13,000-16,000 x g), which is just: set it to maximum and spin.

## The Lab's Centrifuges

### Benchtop Microcentrifuges (x2)

<!-- PHOTO: Our benchtop microcentrifuge (closed, showing controls) -->

* Hold **microcentrifuge tubes** (1.5 mL and 2.0 mL Eppendorf tubes)
* Maximum speed: \~13,000-16,000 RPM (\~16,000 x g depending on model)
* No temperature control (room temperature only)
* Use for: DNA extractions, pelleting debris, quick spins ("pulse spin" to collect liquid at the bottom of a tube)

### Large Refrigerated Centrifuge

<!-- PHOTO: Our large refrigerated centrifuge with rotor visible -->

* Holds **Falcon tubes** (15 mL, 50 mL), **plates** (96-well), and microcentrifuge tubes (with adapter rotor)
* Multiple rotors: fixed-angle rotor for Falcon tubes, swing-bucket rotor for plates, microcentrifuge rotor
* Temperature control: can run at 4C for temperature-sensitive work
* Use for: larger-volume extractions, plate-based protocols, anything that needs to stay cold during centrifugation

## Procedure

### 1\. Balance your tubes

<!-- PHOTO: Properly balanced tubes in a microcentrifuge rotor -->

**This is the most important step.** An unbalanced centrifuge vibrates violently and can be damaged or injure someone.

* Always load tubes in **opposing pairs** of equal weight.
* If you have an odd number of tubes, make a **balance tube**: fill an empty tube with water to match the weight of your sample tube.
* For microcentrifuge tubes with similar volumes, eyeballing is usually fine.
* For the large centrifuge with Falcon tubes, weigh the tubes on the [[top-loading-balance]] and match them within 0.5 g.

### 2\. Load the rotor

* Place tubes in opposing slots. For a 24-slot rotor, tube in slot 1 gets a partner in slot 13.
* Make sure caps are closed and tubes are fully seated.
* For the large centrifuge: select the correct rotor for your tube type. Do not put Falcon tubes in the microcentrifuge rotor.

### 3\. Set speed and time

* Most DNA extraction protocols: **max speed, 5-10 minutes** on the microcentrifuge.
* The protocol you are following will specify speed and time. Follow it.
* If the protocol says ">10,000 x g" and you're using the microcentrifuge, just use max speed.

### 4\. Start the run

* Close the lid. The centrifuge will not start with the lid open.
* Press start.
* **Stay nearby for the first 15 seconds.** If you hear a loud vibration, grinding, or clicking, **stop immediately**. This means the rotor is unbalanced or something is wrong.

### 5\. After the run

* Wait for the rotor to come to a **complete stop** before opening the lid. Never try to stop it with your hand.
* Remove your tubes promptly.
* If you used the refrigerated centrifuge, leave the lid open briefly to prevent condensation.

## Pulse spin

A "pulse spin" or "quick spin" means: close the lid, press start, and immediately press stop. The centrifuge spins up briefly and then decelerates. This takes 5-10 seconds and is used to collect liquid from the walls and cap of a tube to the bottom. 

## Common Errors

| Error | What happens | How to avoid |
| ----- | ------------ | ------------ |
| Unbalanced rotor | Violent shaking, potential damage | Always balance |
| Wrong rotor for tube type | Tubes crack or don't fit | Check before loading |
| Opening lid while spinning | Injury risk | Wait for full stop |
| Forgetting to set temperature (large centrifuge) | Sample degrades | Check temp before starting if protocol requires 4C |
