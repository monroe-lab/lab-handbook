# Clean Bench (Laminar Flow Hood)

**Purpose:** Provide a sterile work environment for plating seeds, pouring plates, and handling sterile cultures.

---

## Background

A laminar flow clean bench blows HEPA-filtered air in a uniform (laminar) flow across the work surface. The HEPA filter removes >99.97% of particles ≥0.3 um — this includes bacteria (~1 um), fungal spores (~2-10 um), and dust that carries them. The positive pressure inside the bench keeps unfiltered room air from entering the work area.

**Clean bench vs. biosafety cabinet:** A clean bench protects your *samples* from contamination (air blows from filter toward you). A biosafety cabinet (BSC) protects *you* from your samples (air is sucked inward, then filtered before exhaust). For Arabidopsis tissue culture, a clean bench is appropriate — you're not working with human pathogens. If you ever work with something hazardous (certain Agrobacterium strains, chemical mutagens), use a BSC instead.

**Why sterile technique matters for plants:** Fungal spores are everywhere — on your skin, in the air, on surfaces. A single spore landing on a nutrient-rich MS plate can produce a visible colony in 2-3 days that rapidly overgrows seedlings. The clean bench, combined with ethanol spraying and aseptic handling, keeps the contamination rate at an acceptable level (typically <5% of plates).

---

## Setup

1. **Turn on the blower** at least 15 min before use (clears airborne contaminants from the work surface)
2. **Turn on UV lamp** for 15-30 min before use (optional but recommended — UV cross-links DNA in surface microbes)
3. **Turn off UV** before working (UV damages skin and eyes — never work under UV)
4. Spray down the work surface with **70% ethanol**, wipe with paper towels
5. Place only what you need inside — minimize clutter (objects create turbulence that disrupts laminar flow)

## Working in the clean bench

- **Spray your gloves** with 70% ethanol before starting
- Keep the sash at the recommended height (marked on the hood)
- Work **toward the back** of the hood — air flows from back to front, so your hands should be downstream of your work
- **Flame or spray** anything entering the sterile field (tube caps, forceps, etc.)
- Don't reach over open plates or tubes (your arm carries contaminants)
- Don't talk, cough, or sneeze toward your work (mouth/nose are major sources of bacteria)

> *Plates keep getting contaminated despite working in the clean bench:* Check that the HEPA filter isn't overdue for replacement, that the blower is actually running at full speed, and that you're not blocking airflow with too much equipment. Also check your ethanol spraying technique — the most common source of contamination is touching non-sterile surfaces and then touching plates.

## After use

1. Remove all materials
2. Spray down surface with 70% ethanol
3. Turn off blower (some labs leave it running — check lab convention)
4. Turn on UV for 15-30 min if desired
